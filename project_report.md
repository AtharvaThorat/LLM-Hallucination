## Abstract
Large Language Models (LLMs) achieve strong performance
in question answering but remain prone to hallucinations, pro-
ducing fluent yet unsupported or incorrect responses. This issue
is particularly critical in the medical domain,  where inaccu-
rate patient-specific information can compromise safety. In this
project, we frame hallucination as a system-level design prob-
lem rather than solely a model limitation; by "system-level" we
mean constraints enforced programmatically outside the model’s
forward pass, including retrieval filtering, evidence thresholding,
and abstention logic. We propose a hallucination-resistant frame-
work that combines Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
with  explicit  evidence  constraints  and  an  abstention  mecha-
nism over structured patient records.  The system is designed
to generate answers only when sufficient supporting evidence
is available; otherwise, it abstains to reduce unsupported out-
puts. We conduct a pilot evaluation against multiple baselines
using hallucination-focused metrics such as hallucination rate,
evidence grounding, and supported answer rate. Current results
are based on Gemini and GPT-5.4 outputs.
## 1    Introduction
In this project, we investigate whether explicit evidence con-
straints can eliminate unsupported claims in medical question
answering without making responses unusably incomplete. We
propose a hallucination-resistant framework that combines retrieval-
augmented generation (RAG) with programmatic evidence con-
straints and an abstention mechanism. The system operates over
structured patient records and generates answers only when suffi-
cient supporting evidence is retrieved; otherwise, it abstains with
responses such as “Insufficient information.” This shifts respon-
sibility for correctness from the model’s parametric knowledge
to an external evidence verification pipeline grounded in patient
data. We compare our approach against full-context prompting,
rule-based retrieval,  and standard semantic RAG, evaluating
across both Gemini and GPT models.   Our contributions in-
clude a modular evidence-constrained pipeline, a hallucination-
focused evaluation setup, and initial results demonstrating re-
duced unsupported content. The remainder of this report covers
our current research progress (Section 2), literature review (Sec-
tion 3), experimental results (Section 4), and updated research
directions (Section 5).
## 2    Current Research Progress
We have implemented a modular patient-centric QA pipeline and
partially evaluated it for hallucination detection and evidence-
grounded answering.  The system separates retrieval, prompt
construction, model invocation, and evaluation so that ground-
ing and assessment are handled explicitly outside the language
model. Initial end-to-end pilot experiments have been run across
multiple LLM backends to validate the pipeline and measure
qualitative behavior of proposed defenses against hallucination.
## 2.1    Dataset
The experimental corpus is a de-identified structured medical
QA dataset containing 200 patient records.  The dataset was
synthetically generated by the authors, with patient records and
QA interactions constructed to reflect realistic clinical scenarios
across a range of question types.  Each record includes struc-
tured  clinical  fields  and  associated  QA  items  with  evidence
annotations. Each patient record contains standard clinical fields
(demographics, past medical history, active medications, docu-
mented allergies, and current symptoms) together with a set of
gold QA interactions.
2.1.1    Gold QA Format
For each QA item the dataset provides:  (i) a question, (ii) a
ground-truth answer, and (iii) fine-grained supporting evidence
entries that explicitly point to the source clinical field and the
exact evidence text. This provenance annotation enables auto-
mated, evidence-aware evaluation of model outputs (measuring
whether a claim is supported by the patient record).
2.1.2    Properties and Suitability
Records  are  strictly  patient-local  (no  mixing  of  information
across patients) and therefore appropriate for medically sensitive
experiments. The dataset is balanced across a variety of clinical
question types (presence/absence queries, medication checks,
allergy contraindications, simple factual retrieval), which per-
mits measuring both hallucination (unsupported claims) and
standard QA metrics (exact match, token F1). Because each QA
item links answers to explicit evidence spans, the data supports
precision/recall style metrics for grounding and can be used for
abstention-aware evaluations.
## 2.2    Completed Tasks
2.2.1    Modular system architecture
We implemented a modular pipeline that enforces separation of
concerns:  (1) patient data loading and chunking, (2) retrieval
(rule-based and semantic), (3) baseline prompt construction, (4)
a unified model invocation layer that can route prompts to dif-
ferent LLM backends, and (5) automated evaluation producing
per-query metrics and aggregate summaries.
2.2.2    Retrieval components
We implemented two retrieval strategies: a rule-based keyword
retriever as an interpretable baseline, and a semantic retriever us-
ing all-MiniLM-L6-v2 (384-dimensional sentence embeddings)
with cosine similarity over atomically chunked patient fields,

retrieving the top k = 5 most relevant chunks per query.
2.2.3    Baselines and proposed method
Three  baselines  were  implemented:  (1)  full-context  prompt
containing  all  patient  fields,  (2)  rule-based  retrieval  prompt
with filtered fields, and (3) semantic RAG using top-k chunks.
The proposed method augments semantic RAG with an explicit
prompt-level constraint instructing the model to answer only if
the retrieved evidence explicitly supports a response; otherwise
the model returns ’Insufficient information’, which is detected
programmatically via keyword matching on a fixed set of absten-
tion phrases.  This enforces evidence grounding at the system
level.
2.2.4    Model integration and pilot runs
A  unified  query  function  was  used  to  run  pilot  experiments
across hosted and local LLMs (commercial API and Hugging
Face models). Pilot runs on held-out subsets validated end-to-
end behavior, response formats, and metric computation.
2.2.5    Evaluation suite
A set of objective metrics was developed and applied per query:
hallucination rate (fraction of model claims unsupported by any
gold evidence), exact match and token-level F1 against gold
answers, evidence precision/recall/F1 (over tokens or spans),
supporting answer rate (SAR), and abstention rate. Aggregation
and visualization utilities were created for cross-model and cross-
baseline comparisons.
## 2.3    Ongoing Work
2.3.1    Performance bottlenecks and mitigation
Large-scale experiments are currently constrained by API rate
limits and per-call latency on hosted LLMs; this increases wall-
clock time and cost for full-cohort evaluation. To address this
we are implementing (a) intelligent request batching and con-
currency control with adaptive rate limiting and exponential
backoff, (b) deterministic response caching to avoid repeated
identical calls during iterative development, and (c) a hybrid ex-
periment strategy that uses local open models for broad sweeps
and hosted APIs for a focused subset of critical comparisons.
2.3.2    Experimental refinements
We are finalizing ablation experiments to quantify sensitivity
to retrieval hyperparameters (chunk length, chunk overlap, em-
bedding model, top-k), prompt template variations (instruction
phrasing and constraint wording), and the evidence threshold
that triggers abstention.  Each ablation will report 95% confi-
dence intervals computed via bootstrap resampling (n = 1000)
over per-query metric scores; this is planned as an extension to
the current aggregation pipeline.
2.3.3    Validation and human review
Automated metrics will be supplemented by targeted human
annotation for ambiguous or high-impact cases to calibrate auto-
matic hallucination detectors and refine evaluation thresholds.
We will also sample model outputs for qualitative error analy-
sis (types of hallucination, partial grounding, hallucinated con-
traindications).
2.3.4    Planned deliverables and timeline (2–4 weeks)
(1) stabilize rate-limit mitigations and run full-cohort experi-
ments using the hybrid strategy, (2) complete ablation studies
and compute aggregate metrics with uncertainty estimates, (3)
produce  figures  and  tables  comparing  baselines  and  models
(hallucination rate, evidence precision/recall, SAR, abstention
trade-offs), and (4) draft a results section with quantitative ev-
idence that the proposed evidence-constrained RAG reduces
unsupported claims at the measured cost of increased absten-
tions.
## 3    Literature Review
The  literature  relevant  to  our  project  falls  into  four  themes:
domain-specialized medical LLMs, retrieval-grounded medi-
cal  QA,  hallucination  detection  and  abstention,  and  faithful
reasoning under evidence constraints.
[1] The paper “Clinical Camel: An Open Expert-Level Med-
ical Language Model with Dialogue-Based Knowledge Encod-
ing” presents an open medical LLM built by fine-tuning LLaMA-
2 with QLoRA and dialogue-based knowledge encoding.   It
shows that domain-specific fine-tuning can substantially im-
prove medical question answering across benchmarks such as
PubMedQA, MedQA, and MedMCQA. This work is important
because it demonstrates the value of adapting general LLMs to
the medical domain, although it focuses more on capability than
hallucination control.
[2] “Toward Expert-Level Medical Question Answering with
Large Language Models” introduces Med-PaLM 2, a medically
specialized model built on PaLM-2 with domain-specific tuning
and refinement.  The paper reports strong benchmark perfor-
mance and physician-level answer quality in human evaluations,
highlighting the promise of large medical LLMs. However, it
also suggests that high capability alone is not sufficient for safe
real-world deployment without additional grounding mecha-
nisms.
[3] “Smoothing Out Hallucinations: Mitigating Hallucina-
tions in LLMs via Softmax Smoothing” studies hallucination
reduction through smoothed knowledge distillation. By reducing
model overconfidence, the method improves factual consistency
while preserving general language understanding performance.
This paper is useful because it treats hallucination mitigation as
a training and calibration problem rather than only a retrieval
problem.
[4] “Towards Reliable Medical Question Answering: Tech-
niques and Challenges in Mitigating Hallucinations in Language
Models”  directly  addresses  hallucination  in  medical  QA.  It
argues that retrieval-augmented generation, explicit evidence
grounding, and abstention when evidence is insufficient are cen-
tral to building reliable systems. This work is especially close
to our project because it frames hallucination as a safety issue
and motivates evidence-constrained answering.
[5] “Benchmarking Retrieval-Augmented Generation for
Medicine  (MIRAGE)”  provides  a  systematic  comparison  of
RAG methods for medical QA. It shows that retrieval can im-
prove answer accuracy significantly, but also notes that retrieving
too much information can introduce noise and hurt performance.
This paper is highly relevant because it supports the idea that
retrieval quality and context selection matter as much as the
model itself.
[6] “MedTrust-RAG: Evidence Verification and Trust Align-
ment for Biomedical Question Answering” proposes a citation-
grounded framework in which answers must be supported by
## 2

evidence and the system refuses to answer when support is in-
sufficient. Its two-agent design separates evidence verification
from answer generation, improving trustworthiness. This paper
closely aligns with our project’s focus on grounded generation
and abstention.
[7] “MEGA-RAG: A Retrieval-Augmented Generation Frame-
work with Multi-Evidence Guided Answer Refinement for Miti-
gating Hallucinations of LLMs in Public Health” extends stan-
dard RAG by combining dense, sparse, and knowledge-graph
retrieval,  followed  by  candidate  comparison  and  refinement.
The main contribution is stronger evidence grounding through
multi-source verification.  This motivates richer retrieval and
verification strategies for reducing unsupported answers.
[8] “DKITNLP at ArchEHR-QA 2025:  A Retrieval Aug-
mented  LLM  Pipeline  for  Evidence-Based  Patient  Question
Answering” focuses on patient-specific QA over clinical notes.
It retrieves minimal sentence-level evidence using BioBERT
retrieval and reranking, then generates grounded answers with
an instruction-tuned LLM. This is very relevant to our work
because it shows how fine-grained retrieval over patient records
can improve traceability and reduce hallucination.
[9] “MedInsight:  A Multi-Source Context Augmentation
Framework for Generating Patient-Centric Medical Responses
Using Large Language Models” combines patient context with
external knowledge to generate personalized medical responses.
Its emphasis is more on patient-friendly explanation and multi-
source context fusion than on strict evidence verification. This
makes it useful as a comparison point for systems that prioritize
helpfulness over strong safety constraints.
[10] “Healthcare Agent: Eliciting the Power of Large Lan-
guage Models for Medical Consultation” studies agentic medical
consultation systems with memory and multi-turn reasoning. It
shows that conversation memory and workflow design can im-
prove follow-up quality, continuity, and safety.  This paper is
relevant because it highlights that reliability can come not only
from model training but also from system design and interaction
structure.
[11] “HalluLens: LLM Hallucination Benchmark” proposes
a  benchmark  that  evaluates  not  just  correctness  but  also  ab-
stention behavior. It argues that a reliable model should know
when to refuse to answer, and therefore hallucination evaluation
must include false refusals and false acceptances. This directly
supports our project’s abstention-aware evaluation.
[12] “LINS: A General Medical Q&A Framework for En-
hancing  the  Quality  and  Credibility  of  LLM-Generated  Re-
sponses” argues that retrieval alone is insufficient unless evi-
dence is filtered,  decomposed,  and explicitly linked to state-
ments. By using statement-citation alignment and multi-agent
decomposition, it improves answer credibility. This paper sup-
ports our emphasis on explicit evidence tracing rather than sim-
ple context stuffing.
[13] “TruthfulQA: Measuring How Models Mimic Human
Falsehoods” is a broader benchmark on model truthfulness. It
shows that larger models may still reproduce common miscon-
ceptions and that truthfulness must be measured separately from
fluency. Although not medical-specific, it is important because
it motivates why factuality and safety need dedicated evaluation
metrics.
[14] “SelfCheckGPT: Zero-Resource Black-Box Halluci-
nation Detection” proposes detecting hallucinations using self-
consistency across multiple sampled outputs. If a model gives
inconsistent answers to the same question, the answer is more
likely to be unreliable. This is useful for our project as a possible
lightweight post-generation hallucination detector.
[15] “Semantic Entropy for Hallucination Detection in LLMs”
introduces semantic entropy as a way to measure uncertainty
at the meaning level rather than the token level.  It shows that
higher semantic uncertainty correlates with hallucination risk.
This paper is relevant because it offers a principled uncertainty
signal that could complement evidence-based abstention.
[16] “Localizing Before Answering: A Hallucination Eval-
uation Benchmark for Grounded Medical Multimodal LLMs”
studies hallucination in multimodal medical QA. It proposes
that models should first localize relevant medical regions before
answering, which improves both grounding and robustness. This
broadens the literature review by showing that hallucination is
also a grounding problem in multimodal settings.
[17] “Reducing Hallucinations of Medical Multimodal Large
Language Models with Visual Retrieval-Augmented Generation”
reduces hallucination by retrieving similar medical images and
associated reports.  By grounding outputs in both visual and
textual evidence, it improves reliability in multimodal medical
tasks.  This paper is useful because it extends the RAG idea
beyond text-only pipelines.
[18]  “MEDSEARCH:  A  Conversational  Agent  for  Real-
Time, Evidence-Based Medical Question-Answering” presents a
multi-LLM medical QA system with real-time evidence retrieval.
It uses task-specific routing and answer selection to improve
consistency and maintain evidence-based reasoning. This paper
is relevant because it shows how retrieval, orchestration, and
verification can work together in practical medical QA systems.
[19] “MedRAG: Enhancing Retrieval-Augmented Genera-
tion with Knowledge Graph-Elicited Reasoning for Healthcare
Copilot” incorporates diagnostic knowledge graphs into RAG
to support more precise and evidence-grounded reasoning.  It
also supports proactive follow-up questioning when information
is incomplete. This paper is important because it suggests that
structured medical knowledge can improve both grounding and
diagnostic support.
[20] “Beyond Correctness: Rewarding Faithful Reasoning
in Retrieval-Augmented Generation” argues that correct final
answers are not enough if the intermediate reasoning is unsup-
ported. It introduces the idea of reasoning faithfulness, requiring
consistency between retrieved evidence, reasoning steps, and
final output.  This is relevant to our project because a medical
QA system should be both correct and evidence-faithful.
[21] “R-Tuning: Instructing Large Language Models to Say
‘I Don’t Know’” explicitly trains models to refuse answering
when knowledge is insufficient.  The paper shows that refusal
behavior can generalize across domains and act as a transfer-
able safety skill. This strongly supports our project’s abstention
mechanism, where refusing to guess is often safer than generat-
ing unsupported medical advice. Overall, prior work shows that
medical QA reliability depends not only on model capability
but also on retrieval quality, evidence verification, abstention
behavior, and uncertainty estimation. Our project is most closely
related to work on evidence-grounded QA with abstention, but
differs in focusing specifically on patient-record-level support.

## 4    Initial Results
We conducted a pilot end-to-end evaluation of the proposed
hallucination-resistant  pipeline  against  three  baselines  using
two held-out patient records from the structured medical QA
dataset (24 model-baseline evaluations total). The pilot aimed
to validate the full system pipeline rather than yield statistically
conclusive results. Initially, due to API rate limits (Gemini free
tier), only the Gemini backend produced valid outputs. Subse-
quently, we also incorporated GPT-based evaluation, enabling
comparison across both Gemini and GPT models. All referenced
figures are auto-generated by the visualization module.
## 4.1    Hallucination Rate
Hallucination rate measures the fraction of responses containing
content unsupported by the ground-truth answer or evidence
passages. Across baselines, both models exhibited low halluci-
nation rates, with Gemini consistently producing 0.00 across all
baselines, while GPT showed a non-zero rate only for the pro-
posed method (0.14). This reflects that while GPT occasionally
generates unsupported content under stricter constraints, Gemini
tends to be more conservative.  These preliminary results sug-
gest that explicit grounding constraints can reduce unsupported
content. Figure 1 illustrates this comparison.

4.2    Answer Quality (Token F1)
On token-level F1, both models performed better on baselines
than the proposed method.   GPT achieved higher F1 scores
across all baselines (Baseline 1: 0.65; Baseline 2: 0.63; Baseline
3: 0.63) compared to Gemini (0.50, 0.50, and 0.58, respectively).
However, for the proposed method, GPT scored 0.44 while Gem-
ini scored 0.14.  This gap reflects both model differences and
a deliberate design trade-off.  The proposed method produces
terse responses (e.g., "Yes"), whereas ground-truth answers are
full clinical sentences. Token F1 penalizes brevity heavily, even
when the answer is factually correct.  This motivates incorpo-
rating semantically aware metrics such as BERTScore in future
evaluations. Figure 2 shows the F1 comparison.
## Figure 2: F1 Score Comparison
4.3    Evidence Grounding (Evidence F1)
Evidence F1 measures how well predicted answers are grounded
in retrieved evidence, computed as the harmonic mean of evi-
dence precision and recall. GPT outperformed Gemini across
all baselines (approximately 0.50-0.51 vs. 0.23-0.39), indicating
stronger alignment with retrieved evidence.  For the proposed
method, GPT achieved 0.33 while Gemini scored 0.00. As be-
fore, this is influenced by response brevity: short answers like
"Yes" contain no content words above the threshold used for
evidence recall, causing scores to drop regardless of factual cor-
rectness. This highlights a limitation of token-overlap evidence
metrics rather than a failure of grounding, which is enforced
structurally through prompt constraints.  Figure 3 shows this
comparison.

4.4    Exact Match, SAR, and Abstention
Exact  match  scores  remained  low  across  all  configurations,
which is expected for open-ended generative QA where outputs
are paraphrases or shortened forms of ground-truth sentences.
Gemini achieved a high exact match score for the proposed
method (0.80), while GPT produced near-zero exact matches
across settings, reflecting stylistic differences in response gen-
eration.  For Supporting Answer Rate (SAR), GPT achieved
higher values across baselines (approximately 0.57, 0.57, and
0.43) compared to Gemini (0.17, 0.17, and 0.33), while both
models showed reduced SAR for the proposed method (GPT:
0.14, Gemini: 0.00).  This indicates that stricter grounding re-
duces verbosity and supporting content in responses. Abstention
behavior was minimal across baselines, with GPT showing a
small abstention rate (0.14) for the proposed method while Gem-
ini remained at 0.00 across all settings. This suggests that GPT is
more likely to abstain under uncertainty, whereas Gemini tends
to provide an answer regardless.  These metrics are expected
to  become  more  discriminative  in  the  full-cohort  evaluation
with unanswerable or ambiguous questions. Figures 4, 5 and 6
capture these distributions.

## 4.5    Qualitative Analysis
For factual presence-or-absence questions (e.g., whether a medi-
cation is documented), all methods produced correct answers,
with baselines generating verbose justifications and the proposed
method responding concisely. A more revealing case arose for
patient PT-46928690:  "Is ibuprofen safe to prescribe for this
patient?" The ground-truth answer cites a documented ibuprofen
allergy causing gastrointestinal bleeding.  All three baselines
were flagged as hallucinating, not for giving the wrong yes/no
answer, but for introducing additional clinical reasoning (e.g.,
NSAID platelet inhibition risks) that, while medically plausi-
ble, was unsupported by the patient record.  This exemplifies
the hallucination category our system targets: fluent, clinically
reasonable content that lacks evidentiary support. The proposed
method stayed within the bounds of retrieved evidence and was
not flagged. This case illustrates why hallucination rate rather
than token F1 is the most meaningful metric for safety-critical
medical Q&A and why a method with lower F1 but zero unsup-
ported content may be preferable in clinical settings.
## 4.6    Summary
The pilot provides preliminary evidence that evidence-constrained
RAG can reduce unsupported content in this small evaluated
subset. The primary cost is reduced response verbosity, which
lowers token-overlap metric scores.  Producing answers that
are both concise and well-grounded while performing well on
standard NLP metrics remains an open challenge that will guide
our ablation studies and metric selection in the full evaluation.
## 5    Updated Research Ideas
Our current system retrieves patient-specific information and
gives it to an LLM to answer medical questions.   Since the

main goal of the project is to reduce hallucinations, our next
research ideas focus on making the system more reliable, better
grounded in evidence, and safer in cases where the patient record
is incomplete or unclear.
1.Better Retrieval Methods:  We want to test different
ways of retrieving patient information,  such as giving
the full patient record, using keyword-based retrieval, or
using vector-based retrieval. This will help us understand
which method gives the most relevant context and reduces
hallucinations the most.
2.Evidence-Based Answering: Another idea is to make the
model answer only when the retrieved patient data clearly
supports the response. This can help reduce answers that
sound correct but are not actually grounded in the record.
3.Abstention When Evidence Is Missing: In medical set-
tings, it may be safer for the system to say “I don’t know”
instead of guessing.   We plan to explore ways for the
model to abstain when the patient data does not contain
enough information to answer confidently.
4.Lightweight Hallucination Detection: To include a training-
based component,  we can build a small classifier that
checks whether a generated answer is supported by the
patient data or is hallucinated. This would allow us to add
training without changing the overall system too much.
## 5.
Verification Layer:  We also want to explore adding a
second step that verifies whether the final answer matches
the retrieved evidence before it is shown to the user. This
could work as an extra safety check.
6.Comparing System Design vs Training:  Finally, we
want  to  compare  system-based  improvements  such  as
retrieval and prompting with lightweight training-based
methods such as a classifier or verifier. This will help us
understand whether better system design alone is enough,
or whether some training is also needed to reduce halluci-
nations effectively.
Overall, our project is still centered on safe medical question
answering, not diagnosis prediction. The main research direction
is to study how retrieval, grounding, abstention, and lightweight
training can work together to make the system more trustworthy.
